The Gateway of India, often referred to colloquially as the "Mumbai Gate," is one of the most iconic landmarks in Mumbai, India. Here is a detailed overview of its history, construction, and additional important facts:

### 1. History
The Gateway of India was constructed to commemorate the visit of King George V and Queen Mary to India in December 1911. During their visit, they arrived at the Apollo Bunder (the waterfront area near the Gateway of India) by ship, and the monument was designed to welcome them. The structure symbolizes the British Raj in India and marked the beginning of a new era of British influence in the subcontinent.

The Gateway has also been a site of significant historical events. After India gained independence in 1947, it became a symbol of the end of British rule. Additionally, it has witnessed various events, including the departure of the last British troops from India in 1948.

### 2. Who Built It
The architectural design of the Gateway of India was the brainchild of the renowned British architect George Wittet. The structure combines elements of Indo-Saracenic and Muslim architectural styles, which reflect the historical relationship between India and the British Empire. 

### 3. Year of Construction
Construction of the Gateway of India began in 1911 and was completed in 1924. The foundation stone was laid on March 31, 1911, and the structure was officially inaugurated on December 4, 1924.

### 4. Important Facts
- **Architectural Features**: The Gateway of India stands 26 meters (85 feet) high and is made of yellow basalt and reinforced concrete. It features an archway that is 15 feet wide and is adorned with intricate decorations and carvings.
  
- **Symbol of Mumbai**: Over the years, the Gateway of India has become one of Mumbai's most recognizable symbols and a major tourist attraction. It is often featured in photos representing Mumbai.

- **Location**: The monument is situated on the waterfront in the Apollo Bunder area, facing the Arabian Sea and situated close to other famous locations in Mumbai, such as the Taj Mahal Palace Hotel and Colaba Causeway.

- **Cultural Significance**: The Gateway is a venue for various cultural events, celebrations, and public gatherings. It is also a starting point for ferries that transport visitors to nearby attractions, such as Elephanta Island.

- **Restorations and Maintenance**: Over the years, the Gateway has undergone several refurbishments to maintain its structural integrity and beauty. The Archaeological Survey of India (ASI) is responsible for its upkeep.

- **Film and Media Appearances**: The Gateway of India has been featured in numerous films, documentaries, and television shows, showcasing its significance as an architectural marvel and a symbol of Indian heritage.

The Gateway of India stands not just as an architectural masterpiece but also as a historical witness to India's colonial past and its journey toward independence.
