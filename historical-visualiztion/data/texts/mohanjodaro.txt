Mohenjodaro, an archaeological site located in present-day Sindh, Pakistan, is one of the most significant cities of the ancient Indus Valley Civilization (IVC), which flourished around 2500 BCE to 1900 BCE. While it is not in India, it is often mentioned in relation to the broader Indus Valley civilization that spanned parts of modern-day India and Pakistan. Here is a detailed overview of Mohenjodaro:

### 1. History
Mohenjodaro, meaning "Hill of the Dead" in Sindhi, was rediscovered in the 1920s. It is one of the largest and most advanced urban settlements of the Indus Valley Civilization, which is known for its urban planning, sophisticated drainage systems, and use of baked brick architecture. The site covers approximately 250 hectares and showcases a well-organized layout featuring residential, administrative, and industrial areas.

The civilization that created Mohenjodaro thrived in the Indus River basin, benefiting from fertile land and trading links with other ancient cultures, including Mesopotamia. However, by around 1900 BCE, the Indus Valley Civilization began to decline for reasons that are still debated among historians and archaeologists. The site of Mohenjodaro was abandoned around this time, and the reasons for its decline could include climatic changes, river shifts, and possibly social upheavals.

### 2. Who Built It
Mohenjodaro was built by the people of the Indus Valley Civilization, which is one of the world's oldest urban cultures. While specific names or individual builders have not been identified, it is believed that the city was the product of a highly organized society with a centralized authority that governed the urban layout, construction, and maintenance of infrastructure. This society engaged in agriculture, trade, and craft specialization, which supported a significant population within the city.

### 3. Year of Construction
Mohenjodaro is believed to have been constructed around 2500 BCE, although the exact timeline can vary based on archaeological findings and differing interpretations. The city reflects advanced urban planning and architectural techniques for its time, suggesting that its construction occurred over multiple phases and involved a significant labor force.

### 4. Important Facts
- **Urban Planning**: Mohenjodaro is famous for its impressive urban layout. The city was designed with well-planned streets laid out in a grid pattern, and houses built from standardized fired bricks. Most houses had access to a sophisticated drainage system, with toilets that emptied into covered drains.
  
- **Civic Organization**: The presence of a citadel and a well-defined public bathing area, often referred to as the "Great Bath," suggests that there were organized religious or civic functions. This highlights the city's social and possibly ritualistic significance.

- **Trade and Economy**: Mohenjodaro was a center for trade, engaging in commerce with other regions, as evidenced by the discovery of seals used for trade, which featured symbols that are yet to be fully deciphered. This indicates the presence of a complex economic system.

- **Cultural Artifacts**: Numerous artifacts, including pottery, jewelry, and tools, have been discovered at the site. The findings suggest a rich culture that valued craftsmanship. Notably, the "Dancing Girl" statuette, a bronze sculpture discovered in Mohenjodaro, exemplifies the artistic skills and aesthetics of the civilization.

- **Script**: The Indus Valley Civilization is known for its undeciphered script, which appears on seals and pottery. The inability to read this script poses challenges in understanding the culture, governance, and social structures of the civilization.

- **UNESCO World Heritage Site**: Mohenjodaro was designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1980, identifying its importance as an archaeological site and its contributions to understanding urbanization in ancient societies.

Mohenjodaro remains a focal point for research into ancient urban cultures and the development of civilization, reflecting the historical depth and complexity of human societies in South Asia.
