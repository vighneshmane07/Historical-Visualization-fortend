### The Taj Mahal

#### 1. History
The Taj Mahal is one of the most iconic structures in the world and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. It is located in Agra, India, on the right bank of the Yamuna River. The Taj Mahal was built as a mausoleum for Mumtaz Mahal, the beloved wife of Mughal Emperor Shah Jahan. Her death during childbirth in 1631 left Shah Jahan devastated, prompting him to commission the Taj Mahal as a tribute to her beauty and memory. The construction of the mausoleum symbolizes the profound love Shah Jahan held for Mumtaz Mahal.

After the completion of the Taj Mahal, Shah Jahan intended to build a second, similar mausoleum in black marble for himself across the river. However, this plan never materialized as he was overthrown by his son Aurangzeb and placed under house arrest, where he spent his final years gazing at the Taj Mahal from his quarters.

#### 2. Who Built It
The Taj Mahal was commissioned by Shah Jahan, the fifth ruler of the Mughal Empire. He was deeply influenced by Persian architecture, and the Taj Mahal combines Islamic, Persian, Ottoman Turkish, and Indian architectural styles. The chief architect is believed to be Ustad Ahmad Lahori, although a team of architects and a large workforce contributed to its construction.

#### 3. Year of Construction
The construction of the Taj Mahal began in 1632 and was largely completed in 1643, although some parts of the complex were finished in subsequent years. The entire project was completed in 1653, taking approximately 21 years and employing thousands of artisans and craftsmen.

#### 4. Important Facts
- **Architectural Style**: The Taj Mahal is known for its stunning white marble façade, which changes color throughout the day. It is characterized by its large dome, minarets, and intricate inlay work using semi-precious stones.

- **Materials Used**: The mausoleum is primarily built from white marble sourced from Makrana in Rajasthan, India. Over 28 different types of precious and semi-precious stones were used for the intricate inlay work, which forms beautiful floral designs and motifs.

- **Symbol of Love**: The Taj Mahal is often referred to as a "symbol of love" due to the emotional story linked to its creation. It attracts millions of visitors from around the world annually, making it a prominent emblem of India's rich history and culture.

- **Garden and Layout**: The complex includes a lush Mughal garden, reflecting pools, and pathways. The layout adheres to the principles of Persian garden design, emphasizing symmetry and harmony.

- **Cultural Impact**: The Taj Mahal has influenced many architectural giants around the world, inspiring numerous replicas and adaptations through the centuries. It has been featured in literature, art, and film, reinforcing its status as a cultural landmark.

- **Conservation Efforts**: Over time, the Taj Mahal has faced challenges such as pollution, environmental factors, and structural changes. Various conservation efforts have been undertaken over the years to preserve its integrity and beauty.

- **Recognition**: In addition to being a UNESCO World Heritage Site, the Taj Mahal is also recognized as one of the New Seven Wonders of the World, a testament to its extraordinary architectural beauty and historical significance.

The Taj Mahal continues to be a stunning example of Mughal architecture and a poignant reminder of love, attracting visitors and scholars alike for its beauty and historical importance.
